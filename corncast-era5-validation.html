Key finding: the chinook warming effect collapses above ~10,300 ft

Warm days on the Front Range (Longmont, Boulder) are driven by downslope foehn winds that warm most intensely at lower elevations. Both SNOTELs sit inside the active chinook zone. The ERA5 grid at 12,858 ft is near the source air — the foehn effect is essentially absent. ERA5 is capturing real atmospheric physics, not an artifact.

Why ERA5 is colder than lapse rate predicts
The standard environmental lapse rate (3.5°F/1000ft) assumes calm, stable air. On warm Front Range days that assumption fails entirely. Chinook/foehn winds descend from the Continental Divide, warming at the dry adiabatic rate (~5.5°F/1000ft) — but the warming concentrates at the lowest elevations where descent has been longest. Niwot at 9,940' and UCamp at 10,330' both sit in the active downslope warming zone. ERA5 at 12,858' is near the divide — it barely participates in the foehn effect. On the 14 warm days Niwot recorded >40°F, ERA5 showed temps 10–23°F colder. This is not ERA5 error — it's real atmospheric physics confirmed by two independent surface observations.
UCamp as a field reference for CornCast
University Camp (#838) at 10,330 ft in the St. Vrain watershed is the best qualitative reference for Indian Peaks summit conditions: • Same watershed drainage as Mt. Toll — matched orographic fetch • 360 ft higher than Niwot — slightly less chinook influence • Only 8 pre-season warm days vs Niwot's 14 • Snow depth data directly validates ERA5 snowfall accumulation Rule of thumb for field planning: UCamp >45°F → ERA5 ~low 30s, no CCC heat depletion UCamp <35°F → Summit 15–25°F, excellent freeze conditions UCamp <20°F → Summit single digits, deep winter quality CornCast using ERA5 directly (not SNOTEL + lapse) is the most accurate approach available.
Feb 5 — Season's most extreme chinook
Feb 5, 2026 was the warmest day at both SNOTELs: Niwot 9,940': 48.0°F (+8.0° above CCC threshold) UCamp 10,330': 45.7°F (+5.7° above threshold) ERA5 12,858': 29.3°F (−10.7° below threshold) The two SNOTELs differed by only 2.3°F over 390 ft — both fully inside the chinook. Then ERA5 dropped 16.4°F over the next 2,528 ft. If CornCast had used Niwot + lapse correction, it would have estimated the summit at ~37.8°F — triggering false CCC heat depletion on one of the coldest summit days of the season. Across all 8 days UCamp exceeded 40°F, lapse-correction would have generated phantom heat depletion every time. ERA5 correctly showed sub-40°F for all 63 days in the dataset.
Data quality and caveats
SNOTEL warm bias: NRCS acknowledges a systematic warm bias in Colorado's post-2004 extended-range sensors. Real SNOTEL temps may be 1–3°F lower than reported, which would further reduce the already-zero pre-season warm day count at summit elevation. ERA5 spatial resolution: ~31km grid cells. The specific grid point sits at 12,858' — only 121' below Mt. Toll's 12,979' summit. Lapse correction is 0.4°F (negligible). Snow depth agreement: UCamp showed 14–34" depth all season, consistent with the 62% of median SWE visible in CornCast's display. ERA5 snowfall accumulation matches the progression. Both datasets tell the same story. Conclusion: Three independent data sources confirm this was a cold, dry pre-season with zero warm penetration at summit elevation. The 95% cold reserve reading is well-supported.
